## Purpose

Canonical page for recurring distractor families in TCF-style QCM.
This page should grow across future sessions.

## Pattern 1: same semantic field, different role

Example family:
- `journaliste`
- `rédacteur en chef`
- `auditeur`

Trap logic:
- all options sound plausible because they belong to media
- only one option matches the exact role in the sentence

Repair move:
- identify the function, not just the topic domain
- ask who produces, who supervises, and who receives

## Pattern 2: near-neighbor lexical contrast

Example family:
- `polluant` vs `nuisible`
- `crue` vs `inondation`
- `émission` vs `programme`
- `numéro` vs `rubrique`

Trap logic:
- one option is broader, another narrower, another describes the consequence instead of the cause

Repair move:
- ask what exactly the sentence needs: category, subtype, event, consequence, or function

## Pattern 3: grammar frame mismatch

Example family:
- `ce qui` / `ce que` / `dont`
- `si + plus-que-parfait / conditionnel passé`

Trap logic:
- distractors are grammatically similar and familiar
- the wrong answer often sounds acceptable until you test the verb structure carefully

Repair move:
- identify the verb pattern before choosing
- decide subject vs direct object vs `de`-complement
- in hypotheses, check whether the clause is unreal past

## Pattern 4: real-world referent gap disguised as vocabulary

Example family:
- `essence sans plomb`
- `conteneur`
- `verre usagé`

Trap logic:
- the failure is not only translation but weak understanding of the thing in the real world

Repair move:
- define the object or process concretely first
- only then map the French label to it

- use this page to design warm-ups and review drills
- when a new mistake repeats across modules, add it here and link to the canonical vocabulary or grammar page
